# Summer School Abstract

## Revised 150-word version

Large language models are increasingly used as advisors in morally consequential situations, yet their judgments can shift across logically equivalent framings, including action-omission and yes-no formulations. This project investigates whether such moral framing biases can be reduced at the model level and whether improved AI advice may later transfer to human moral judgment. In a computational study, I evaluated standard single-response outputs, structured multi-persona debate prompts, and fine-tuned Qwen3-4B variants on moral dilemmas and sycophancy-style tasks. Preliminary results suggest that model-level debiasing can substantially reduce omission and yes-no bias on held-out benchmark items, although generalisation to newly generated dilemmas remains limited. Building on these findings, the next stage will test whether exposure to less biased AI advice reduces human framing sensitivity, changes confidence and trust, and affects reliance on AI recommendations. The project frames LLM moral reasoning as bounded, strategy-dependent decision-making within human-AI interaction.

## Positioning Notes

- This version presents the project as completed model-level work plus a planned human-AI transfer study.
- It avoids implying that the human participant experiment has already been conducted.
- It includes preliminary findings without overloading the abstract with numerical results.
